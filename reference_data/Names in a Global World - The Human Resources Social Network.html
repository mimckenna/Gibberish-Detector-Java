Names in a Global World

By: 
Date: November 29 2001
Introduction
Globalization means HR must learn about other cultures. One of the oft-overlooked basics is how names are structured.

Here are some pointers on understanding the structure of names around the world.

Contents
We cover:
Western Names
Spanish Names
Chinese Names
Thai Names
Muslim Names
Icelandic Names
Sikh Names
Sri Lankan Sinhalese Names
Sri Lankan Tamil Names
If there is a group not covered that you need help with let me know and I''ll look into adding them to this article. Better yet, if your culture is not covered why not write up a section about the structure of names in your culture and send it to: (missopensource@hr.com)

1. Western Names

Sample names:
Male: John Wilson Smith
Female: Marie Anne Dion

You can usually, but not always, determine the gender from the person''s first name.

Formal Addressing:
In formal circumstances one would normally use a prefix followed by the last name. For example for English people:

Male: Mr. Smith
Unmarried Female: Miss Dion or Ms. Dion
Married Female: Mrs. Dion or Ms. Dion

The prefix varies from country to country, for example in France it would be:

Male: M. Smith (Monsieur Smith)
Unmarried Female: Mlle Dion (Mademoiselle Dion)
Married Female: Mme Dion (Madame Dion)

However, in most cases using the English prefixes is acceptable until you learn the individuals´ preference.

Legal Name Changes
It is quite common for a woman to change her last name when she gets married. For example, if Miss Marie Anne Dion married a Mr. John Wilson Smith she might change her name to Mrs. Marie Anne Smith.

Alternatively, she might use a hyphenated name, Mrs. Marie Anne Dion-Smith.

Informal Addressing
Typically Westerners are called by their first names, for example John Wilson Smith would be addresses as John; Marie Anne Dion would be addressed as Marie.

Nicknames
Nicknames are common and are usually related to the first name. For example: David is often called Dave
William is often called Bill
Margaret is often called Meg
Dorothy is often called Dot

2. Spanish Names

Male: Juan MartÂ­nez Escudero
Female: Ana Carri"n Botella

Formal Addressing:
Following an ancient tradition, when a child is born, he or she receives the first surname from the father and the second surname is the first surname of the mother. Portuguese speaking countries also use two family names, but in this case the mother''s surname comes first. For example: Juan MartÂ­nez Escudero + Marta Villanueva Cortés = Juan MartÂ­nez Villanueva. The name structure gets more complicated than this with compound family names; compound first names, mixed first names and nicknames.

Legal Name Changes:
So that a woman never loses her maiden name (as women want their maiden names to be carried through the generations), she adds her husband´s name after her surname.

Informal Addressing:
Juan MartÂ­nez Escudero would be called Juan; while Ana Carri"n Botella would be called Ana.

Nicknames:
As names are very long, people use nicknames (pretty much like Liz is to Elisabeth, Bob is to Robert, or Bill is to William). Family and friends only use nicknames, so it´s suggested not to use them if you are trying to make business with someone or if that person is the president of a State.

(All information is gathered from http://www.stanford.edu/~molinero/html/surname.html#Motivation)

3. Chinese Names

Sample names:
Ji Yi Zhang
Fan Chi Hang

Formal Addressing:
Chinese names are structured so that one´s family name appears first when written and the given names appear last.

For example: If you have an employee whose name is Zhang Ji Yi, Zhang is the last name and Ji Yi are the first or given names. Media commonly get names like this wrong when they put a prefix in front of the name. For example: it would be incorrect to write, "Ms. Yi", as the last name of this person is Zhang, not Yi.

Whereas in Western names one can commonly tell the difference in gender, it is not as common to guess someone''s gender from a Chinese name.

Legal Name Changes:
Women who marry take their husband´s name and place it in front of their last name, so using the two names we mentioned before: If Zhang Ji Yi married Fan Chi Hang, her name would be Fan Zhang Ji Yi. The structure would be husband´s last name first, followed by the rest of the name.

Informal Addressing:
A friend would speak of Zhang Ji Yi using the names, "Ji Yi" not "Ji".

Nicknames:
True story: "Tan Seng Bee? There''s no Tan Seng Bee here!" I said with confidence. Charlie Tan was sitting beside me and gently pointed out he was Tan Seng Bee. Many Chinese take Western first names for everyday conversation, but their legal name remains Chinese. It''s terribly embarrassing when you don''t even know the names of own your co-workers.

4. Thai Names

Sample names:
Male: Kriengsak Niratpattanasai
Female: Charoenrasamee Shinawatra

Formal Addressing:
Thai given names come first, before the family name, as in Western countries.

Surnames are only used for formal or official greetings, or to avoid confusion if they meet someone with the same first name.

As titles and ranks are very important in addressing Thais, use a person''s title or rank plus his or her given name when possible. If Anuwat Wattapongsiri is a physician, he should be addressed as "Doctor Anuwat."

Thai given names are preceded by the courtesy title Khun (meaning Mr., Mrs. or Miss), unless the person has a military, professional or academic title. Khun is used for men and women, married or single. If you do not know a person''s name, simply address them as Khun. Anuwat Wattapongsiri is addressed as "Khun Anuwat."

It is appropriate for a visitor to use Mr., Mrs., or Miss followed by a Thai''s family name in formal situations.

It would be virtually impossible to count all the variations of names, as each child is named specially at birth. The names are often invented and the mother is in many cases the only person to know its meaning.

When doing business in Thailand it is important to know both the nickname and full name of your main contacts.

Legal Name Changes:
By law, married women must change their last names to their husbands'', as is the common practice.

Informal Addressing:
Thais simply use their first name or nickname when addressed informally.

Nicknames:
Every Thai person has a nickname, which they use socially. These nicknames either came from something their family called them from their childhood, or it is a shortened version of their first name (some Thai names can be 35 characters long). (Information gathered from www.thailandday.com)

5. Muslim Names
True Story: The clerk called, "Is Yusof here? Yusof?" Zuraidah sat quietly wondering where this guy Yusof was. I had to nudge her, "They are talking about you!" Her name was Zuraidah binte Yusof, which literally means Zuraidah daughter of Yusof. The Western clerk thought he was calling for a Ms. or Mr. Yusof however, to Zuraidah, Yusof simply her Dad''s name.

Sample Names:
Male: Ibrahim bin Abdul Aziz
Female: Zaynab binte Ali

Formal Addressing:
There are many variations to naming conventions across the Muslim world. HR managers will do well to learn the specifics of the individuals they are dealing with. However, it''s common for Muslim''s not to have a last name as such. Names are of the form:

Zaynab binte Ali which means Zaynab daughter of Ali, and Ibrahim bin Abdul Aziz means Ibrahim son of Abdul Aziz.

Ibrahim´s son Zulkifli would be Zulkifli bin Ibrahim, not Zulkilfi bin Adbul Aziz. As such there are no "last names".

Sometimes you will see longer names such as Saud bin Abdul al-Aziz bin Saud Al Saud al-Kabir where more than one ancestor is referenced.

Legal Name Changes:
Women do not change their name upon marriage.

Informal Addressing:
Zaynab binte Ali would be called Zaynab; while Zulkifli bin Ibrahim would be called Zulkifli.

Nicknames:
Nicknames are common and are often related to the first name. For example, Ziauddin would likely be known to his friends as "Din".

Formal Addressing:
Most Islanders still follow the ancient tradition of deriving their last name from the first name of their father. For example: Helgi HÂ¡lfdanarson is the son of HÂ¡lfdan GuÂ°j"nsson (arson = son).

Legal Name Changes:
A female would usually keep her last name - for example, a woman named HerdÂ­s Pétursd"ttir would keep this name when she married. Pétursd"ttir means "daughter of Pétur" (d"ttir = daughter). Icelanders are always referred to by their given name, not by their surname, which is never used alone.

Informal Addressing:
Helgi HÂ¡lfdanarson would be called Helgi; HerdÂ­s Pétursd"ttir would be called HerdÂ­s.

Nicknames were frequently used for easier identification, some of which later became surnames. They could be complimentary or derogatory - depending upon who or what gave them. Famous nicknames include: Fairhair, Finehair, Tarrybreeks, The Saint, The Red, Bareleg, Cripplehand, The Deep-minded.

7. Sikh Names

Sample Names:
Male: Ravjot Singh Pasricha
Female: Baljit Kaur Sidhu

Formal Addressing:
You could call Ravjot Singh Pasricha, Mr. Singh, but more properly you would refer to him as Mr. Pasricha. Baljit Kaur Sidhu would be known as Mrs. Sidhu.

Informal Addressing:
Ravjot Singh Pasricha would informally be called Ravjot, while Baljit Kaur Sidhu would be called Baljit.

Legal Name Change:
Sikh women may or may not change their surname upon marriage.

If Sikhs are baptized, they may legally change their surname to Singh Khalsa (male) or Kaur Khalsa (female). So a Ravjot Sidhu might become Ravjot Singh Khalsa.

As with Chinese names, the Sikh culture does not recognize gender differences in names, so both Ravjot and Baljit could be either a male or female.

Nicknames:
Nicknames totally depend on the person using them. Like the Chinese, some Sikhs might use a Western nickname to make it simpler for Western people to pronounce. Or, some people might shorten their given name.

For example: Ravjot might use a Western name like, "Dan" as his nickname, or simply shorten his name to Rav.

8. Sri Lankan Sinhalese Names
Sri Lanka has always comprised a mix of ethnic groups consisting of the majority Sinhalese, and the minority Tamil, as well as, Moor (Muslims of Arab origin), Malay (Muslims of Malay origin), Burgher (Anglo-Sri Lankan) and Christians.

Sample Names:
Male: MuhandiramlaGE Simon Female: Kubarna Anandaragah

Formal Addressing:
The Sinhalese community usually has two names. The first is called the "GE" (Sinhalese for House or Tribe, pronounced "gay"), while the second is the actual name of the individual.

Hence a person may be called "MuhandiramlaGE Simon" which indicates that he hails from the "House of Muhandiram" and his name is Simon. This prefix "GE" name is carried down through all descendants irrespective of whether they are male or female and would serve more effectively for searching such family connectivity.

Informal Addressing:
MuhandiramlaGE Simon would be called Simon, while Kubarna Anandaragah would be called Kubarna.

Legal Name Changes:
Sinhalese women usually adopt the second name of the spouse after marriage; yet keep their prefixed family or "GE" name in tact. The Sinhalese usually use an initial to indicate the first name rather than spell it out in its full form.

9. Tamil Names
Sample Names
Male: (Male name usually end in "an") Maaran Valavan
Female: (usually end in "i") Avvai Chevan

Formal Addressing:
In the Tamil community there is no family name structure. Instead, both women and men have a first name and then use their father´s first name as a second name.

For example: Avvai is a woman´s first name and Chevan would be her father´s first name.

Informal Addressing:
Younger people are commonly be called by their first name while older people must not be called by their first names. Instead, they are called Brother, Uncle, and Aunt, etc. If you do not know the person whom you are addressing, you must called them Sir, or Iya and Madam, or Amma.

Legal Name Changes:
When married, women commonly use their husband´s name.

10. Hindu Names

Submitted by Anita V.

Sample Names:
Male Sample Name: Rajiv Kapoor
Female Sample Name: Seema Pai

Formal Addressing:
In India we follow the British system to a large extent and in cities especially at workplaces, you will find Mr./Mrs./Ms. as common ways of formally addressing people.

Traditionally, according to the Indian system, a:

  • Hindu Indian married male would be addressed as Shriman,

  • Hindu Indian married female would be addressed as Shrimati

  • Hindu Indian unmarried male would be addressed as Kumar

  • Hindu Indian unmarried female would be addressed as Kumari
India has hundreds of languages, which are spoken across states and the forms of addressing people varies from state to state. I have given you examples from the language Hindi which is our national language and is widely used all over the country, apart from English which is still extremely popular and is the commonly used language for business interactions.

Informal Addressing:
Informal names are short forms of one''s own name or could be a special family name. Take my name; I''m called Ani (short for Anita) by my friends and some of my colleagues, while the others call me Anita. Normally the first name is commonly used for addressing a person informally. To give you another example: Rajiv Kapoor would be addressed as Rajiv informally and as Mr. Kapoor formally. In a traditional setting he would be called Shriman Rajiv if he was married and Kumar Kapoor if he were very young and unmarried.

Families normally shorten the given name. To give you an example: Vikram would be called Vicky (Male) and Neelavati would be called Neela (Female).

It is not always necessary for your nickname to be related to your given name; you could be given a nickname, which has nothing to do with your name. For example, names like Pinky, Sweety, etc. are common in some states in North India. Some people might have very complicated long Indian names and would have another simpler version, which would be used by family and friends.

To give you an example, Veeramani Karthikeyan Sundarajan would be called "Sundar" for short by his family, friends and colleagues. A lot of names (especially in the South of India) are names of Hindu gods, or a combination of Hindu gods, so they can be long and complicated.

Of late, the trend seems to be towards naming children with Sanskrit (said to be the mother of all languages) words. Indians seem to be going back to their roots as far as names are concerned. Western names which used to be hugely popular are quite passé now.

Legal Name Changes:
Normally women change their surnames and take on their husband''s name after marriage. These days there are a number of women (and their tribe is increasing) who choose to retain their maiden names even after they get married, or as given in your example, add their husband´s name to their existing surname.

Nicknames:
Since we''re such a large and diverse country, names vary from state to state, and so do nicknames. By and large, a short form of your name would be one''s nickname: For example, Anu for Anuradha (Female) and Gopal for Gopalan (Male).

A Complication: Titles
In the United States it is uncommon to refer to someone by their title although it is not unknown. For example, "Officer, I really didn''t see the red light" or "Mr. President here is my report on world trade." In other parts of the world titles are more common. In Malaysia, someone in your office might be referred to as "Tuan Hajji", this is not their name, merely a respectful term for a man who has completed the Hajj pilgrimage. You will need to be alert for occasions where people are referred to by title rather than by name.

Conclusion
It´s good form for an HR person to understand the name structure of their employees. If you are a global business then it goes beyond "good form" to being essential knowledge.

Of course, I have missed many cultures, so I encourage you to send me more for a follow-up article.
